Spirit ually, stress can change a person's general outlook on life.① Many illnesses, such as hyper tension, gastric ulcers, bronchial asthma, and coronary heart disease havebeen linked to stress.
Stress initiates stress response. A successful response to the stressor is termed adaptation.
A dap tation would be anyway the bodytries to assist the individual to meet needs and maintain homeostasis.②
Hans Selye, who was known as the" father of stress", proposed the general adaptation syndrome (GAS) to describe individual's biochemical mechanism of adapting to stress. He suggested that regardless of what caused the stress, the human reaction occurred in three distinct stages: alarm reaction stage, stage of resistance or adaptation, and the stage of exhaustion ③. In the alarm reaction stage, the body becomes aware of the stressor and prepares to fight. The prominen t bodily change at this time is the increase of the hormonal levels. In the stage of resistance or adaptation, the body attempts to repair any damage caused by the stress and the body'sadap tation to the stressor takes place during this stage. When the body is successful in its fight against the threat, the GASends with the stage of resistance as homeostasis is restored. If the stage of resistance is not successful, the body may enter the stage of exhaustion. This stage, a prolonged state of preparedness, depletes the body's energy store and eventually its ability to maintain resistance to the t hreat. Without ou tside in terven tion, the outcome of the stage of exhaustion is illness or even death.
So far, researches on stress indicate that stress is essentially the adjustive demand placed on the body. When excessive, stress over taxest he adaptive resources of the individual and eventually leads to a breakdown in functioning. However, a moderate level of stress can improve the performance and functional efficiency of the body and is necessary and desirable for optimal human functioning.
